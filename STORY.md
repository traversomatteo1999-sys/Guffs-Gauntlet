# Guff's Gauntlet — Chapter I: Story Bible & Build Brief

> **Dual-purpose file.** This is both the **narrative bible** (what the chapter means, who everyone is) and a **build brief** (what to add to `SPEC.md` to make it real).
> **Division of labor:** this file owns the *words and the why*; `SPEC.md` owns the *mechanics*. When extending SPEC, use **§8 (the four levels)** and **§12 (build map)** as your task source.
> **Tags:** **[WORKING]** = placeholder name, swap freely · **[CALL]** = a decision made for you, open to override · **[TBD]** = still yours to decide (all collected in §11).

---

## 1. Premise (one sentence)

You are a planeswalker who comes to a foreign plane to free a hero and topple a tyrant — and you slowly learn that the hero will *become* the tyrant the instant he wins, because the prize they are both reaching for turns whoever holds it into the very thing you came to destroy. Your only real victory is to make sure **no one** holds it — even your friends, even if it leaves the world undefended.

---

## 2. The lesson (the spine — every beat serves this)

**You do not end an extreme by becoming a worse one.** Fight fire with fire and you are left with a bigger fire.

The Dominion rules by the Ember. The Conclave would rule by the Ember. The way out of the cycle is not to *win* the Ember — it is to deny it to everyone. The player, an outsider from another plane with nothing to gain here, is the only one who can make that choice cleanly.

This is the horseshoe the chapter teaches: two warring sides that look like opposites and are the same machine — fascism and communism arriving at the same camps; a "liberation" exported at gunpoint for someone's gain. The tragedy is that the side you help is **sincere**. Guff is not lying. He truly believes that holding ultimate power forever is how you keep everyone safe forever. That is exactly why he has to be stopped.

## 3. The Ember (the heart) — and the power-curve solution

**The app icon is the thesis statement:** a black gauntleted hand cradling a burning vortex. That is the Ember. *"Guff's Gauntlet"* reads two ways — the trials you run, **and** a hand closing around the Ember. The whole chapter is about who holds it.

**What it is.** **Vael's own creation** — his masterwork. He *forged* it: a flame of deathless fire, the source of his power and the binding of his immortality. He and the Ember are bound; it is his to a degree no later holder can fully claim. **[CALL: a relic Vael himself made; its forging was the war's *turning point* — it is what broke the Conclave.]**

**What it does.**
- **Rebirth / near-immortality** — "the embers choose a new shape." Its holder does not stay dead.
- **Raw power** — it can be *wielded*, not just worn. Guff covets it because he knows how to unlock its full use.
- **Temptation** — what it *offers*, permanent absolute power, corrupts whoever carries it: *keep me, and you could fix everything, forever.* It made Vael; it will reach for Guff; and it reaches for **you** the moment you carry it (L3–L4) — which is how the player learns the lesson firsthand instead of being told it.

**Two canon rules (these tie mechanic to lore — enforce them):**
1. **Two phases = the Ember's rebirth. Only the holder gets a second life.** → Vael *with* the Ember (L1) is two-phase and cannot truly be killed; Vael *without* it (L4) and Guff (never holds it) are **single-phase mortals**.
2. **The bond severs when someone else takes it.** Vael stays deathless — even after hiding the Ember — until the **player claims it in L3.** That act renders Vael mortal *and* transfers the Ember's effects (and the retry mechanic) to the player.

**Destruction is clean [CALL].** Shattering it simply ends the deathless power and denies it to everyone. There is no in-game *choice* at that moment (the finale is fixed), so we do **not** load the act with an agonizing weighed cost. The world is not literally doomed — Guff's fury is **grief over a lost guarantee of safety**, not "you've killed us all." The grayness lives entirely in **Guff's sincerity.**

### The power-curve solution (why beating full-power Vael in L1 doesn't break the finale)
The worry: "If I defeat Vael at full power *with the Ember* in L1, I'm too strong for everything after, and Guff is a fool to fight me." Resolved on two fronts:

- **It's narrative, not mechanical.** In a deck-vs-deck game, difficulty is set by the *opponent's* deck and the level's rules — not a persistent player stat. L1 and L4 can be tuned to any relative difficulty; nothing forces the finale to be easy.
- **The Ember is the real final boss; people are mortal once cut off from it.**
  - **L1 is not a flex — it's a failure you survive.** Vael holds the Ember, so he *cannot be killed*; you weather him and force him to relight elsewhere while you free Guff. "Couldn't kill the deathless thing, escaped with the prisoner" is the opposite of "I'm unstoppable."
  - **The finale is winnable *because* you destroyed the Ember.** Counterfactual: had Guff *gotten* it, he'd be the eternal, unbeatable tyrant — strictly worse than Vael. Shattering it drags the last fight down from impossible to merely very hard. The beatable finale is the *reward* for your right choice.
  - **Guff isn't foolish — he seizes his one window.** He's not a lone deathless tyrant; he's the entire reborn Conclave at peak strength, a military mind with two lieutenant-commanders and a planeswalker engine — and he strikes at the exact instant you've thrown away the only thing that made you untouchable.

## 4. The world — one **plane** — **[WORKING: Ashveil]**

**MTG terminology:** in Magic, a **plane** *is* a world (synonymous); "realm" is just flavor, not a unit — so this bible uses **plane**. The Warren of Embers and the Drowned Conclave are **two powers on the same plane** (one world, two warring nations, as on Ravnica or Dominaria). The one element from **off-plane** is **you**, a planeswalker.

- **The Ember Dominion — Vael — R/B.** Rule by fire, fear, and decay; openly brutal; everyone is fuel. **Won** the war; holds the Ember. Seat: **the Warren of Embers**, where the conquered are thrown.
- **The Conclave / Drowned Conclave — Guff — W/U/R.** Once a maritime empire of "enlightenment" — fleets that brought order and reason, i.e. conquest and forced civilization. The Dominion's war broke them, sank the fleet, and **drowned their capital** — hence the remnant hides in the flooded ruin, and hence the rank **Commodore**. Fire holding the drowned sea-lord: the shape of the war.
- **The gray (handle with restraint).** The Conclave's imperial past is **true** but **not foregrounded** — no early "secretly evil" telegraph. In the present they read as sympathetic underdogs and Guff reads as a genuine hero; the darkness is latent and crystallizes only when Guff reaches for the Ember at the end of L4.

### The player
A **planeswalker** — a being with a **spark** who travels the **Multiverse** between planes. You walked in from another plane when the Conclave's plight drew you; you *arrived* (not "summoned" like a conjured creature) and can **planeswalk home** when it's done. **Deck-agnostic by design:** your magic and commander are your own, run to run, and nothing leans on your colors. You have **no throne to inherit and nothing to gain here** — which makes your final refusal of the Ember *pure.* You are the one person with no reason to keep it.

### Commodore Guff — W/U/R, Jeskai (Superfriends)
Founder and supreme commander of the Conclave — which is why you free **him** first; he's the keystone of the rebuild. **A hero for the whole chapter until the final beat:** gracious, brilliant, principled, sincerely devoted to ending the cycle. Jeskai is the point — law + insight + zeal: conviction, discipline, fervor. **Dangerous *because* he is motivated.** He reaches for the Ember not from greed but from sincere conviction that holding ultimate power forever is how you make a plane permanently safe — and, of course, permanently the Conclave's. **Final boss: single-phase mortal** (no Ember), playing a **Superfriends planeswalker deck** helmed by the **double-commander** of the two rescued generals. His fury is **grief and conviction, not villainy.**
**Appearance:** red — a raft of reddish hair and an aggressive red beard (canon Commodore Guff); in Ashveil, broad-shouldered and scarred by the war.

### Vael, the Ember Tyrant — R/B → B/R/G
**The victor of the war that broke your order**, and your L1 obstacle because he holds Guff caged. He holds the Ember **because he forged it** — hence deathless, hence two-phase, hence he escapes L1. **A tragic artificer, not just a monster:** he made the Ember "to end the wars" and became what you see — so his dying words in L4 land as a warning pointing straight at Guff (*"I forged it to end the wars. Watch your Commodore reach for the hammer."*). In **L4 he returns as a Jund (B/R/G) army deck** — weakened to mortality by the loss of the Ember, but at the head of his fully mobilized horde, leaning into raw green swarm and primal fury to reclaim what was taken. Keep **every existing L1 line verbatim** and let context flip the meaning (*"Embers do not choose twice"* now reads as a curse about the Ember's next victim).

### Conclave generals A (W/U) & B (G/W)
The scattered leaders you **rescue in L2** to rebuild the order — and, in the cruelest turn, the two who stand with Guff in the finale as his **double-commander.** The people you saved, now against you. One is a planeswalker, one a legendary creature.

### Grakk & Murglax — L1 wardens
The Ember Gate's gatekeeper (Grakk, mono-R) and the Slag Pit's attrition tyrant (Murglax, mono-B). **Existing content, unchanged** — decks, intros, sigils, death-lines all kept.

### The Conclave (as a body)
Your handlers and home base in the Drowned ruin between levels — the cause you serve, bond with, and finally have to stop.

## 6. The anti-telegraph rule (how the turn stays earned)

No winks, no cracks in Guff. For the whole chapter he is simply a hero and the Conclave is simply worth saving. **The only foreshadowing is the Ember itself:** you *feel its pull* once you carry it (L3–L4), and *dying Vael warns you* (L4). The lesson is taught **in the player's own hands**, not preached by a bystander — so when Guff asks for the Ember, the player already knows in their body what it means, and the turn lands as tragedy, not a reveal.

## 7. Modes & power-curve (product shape) — **[CALL: two modes; overridable]**

You do not have to choose between "board game" and "adventure": an adventure made of discrete, self-contained boards *is* a board-game campaign (cf. Planechase planar runs, campaign board games, story-roguelikes). The gauntlet is the chassis for the story.

- **Endless / Gauntlet mode** — the existing experience, kept: pick a boss, run it forever, pure board-game challenge, no narrative commitment.
- **Campaign / Chapter mode** — the four-level adventure, played through with the lore. Replayable in its own right, because you bring a **different deck** each run.

One app, two front doors; same boards, same engine. Future chapters slot in as new campaigns without touching Endless mode.

**Power-curve mechanic to preserve:** the **Ember retry net** (a lost battle retries that battle, not the whole level) is active while the player carries the Ember — i.e. **present for the Vael horde (L4 Stage 1) and gone the moment you destroy it (L4 Stage 2, the Conclave).** That asymmetry *is* the power-curve solution made playable: your hardest fight is the one where you've just given up your safety net. Keep it.

## 8. The four levels (narrative + spec-actionable)

### Level 1 — Guff's Liberation · **DUNGEON** *(already built — re-lore only)*
- **Setting / mode:** Vael's Warren of Embers; the existing three-boss dungeon descent.
- **Enemies:** Grakk (R) → Murglax (B) → **Vael (R/B, two-phase, holds the Ember).**
- **What happens:** drawn to a losing war, you descend to break the Conclave's founder from the tyrant's hold. You leave certain you've done good.
- **Ember role:** establishes Vael's deathlessness; his escape *is* the rebirth.

- **Stage 1 — Vael's horde:** fight **some Dominion generals** [TBD] and the horde, then **mortal Vael — now Jund (B/R/G)**, a different, stronger *army* deck (in lore: his full host, marched to reclaim the Ember). **Kill Vael forever** (single-phase, no Ember).
- **The turn (between stages):** Vael's dying warning → **Guff asks for the Ember** before you go home ("with it the Conclave keeps the whole plane safe forever") → **you destroy the Ember** (`player_has_ember = false`; retry net switches off) → the Conclave turns on you.
- **Stage 2 — final trial, The Conclave:** fight **Guff (W/U/R Jeskai Superfriends)** helmed by the **double-commander of the two rescued generals** (one planeswalker, one creature). Single-phase mortal; hard *because* you just disarmed yourself.
- **Emotional beat:** triumph → the ask → heartbreak → the lesson said aloud by a man you respect, who does not deny being Vael's mirror.
- **Build note:** see §7 for the retry-net asymmetry (net for Stage 1, none for Stage 2). The two co-commanders are the L2 generals reused.

## 9. The ending (authored, bleak, franchise-hinged)

You defeat Guff. The Dominion is leaderless, the Conclave broken again, the Ember gone — for one breath the plane is free of every tyranny at once. But power abhors a vacuum, and you are the one left standing where the throne was. The final image is the temptation of that empty seat — and your **refusal**: a planeswalker with no claim and nothing to gain, you turn and walk away, the only one who will not become the next ember to "choose a new shape." The cycle is not **broken**, only **declined** — and somewhere else on the plane, something is already moving to fill the vacuum. That is the hook into the next **gauntlet.**

5. **L2** = rescue a **W/U** general + a **G/W** general, then a **siege** as the final trial.

7. **L4** = Vael's horde (**Vael now B/R/G Jund**, mortal) → destroy the Ember → fight the Conclave (Guff, **Superfriends**, **double-commander** of the two L2 generals: one planeswalker + one creature).
8. **Two canon rules:** two phases = Ember rebirth (so L4 Vael & Guff are single-phase); the bond severs when the player claims the Ember (L3).
9. **Product shape** = two modes (keep **Endless**, add **Campaign**).
10. **Player** = deck-agnostic off-plane planeswalker; origin in the intro; can planeswalk home.
